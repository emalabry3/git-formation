SUMMARY OF CYRANO DE BERGERAC
Play by Edmond Rostand (1897)

== GENERAL OVERVIEW ==

Cyrano de Bergerac is a play in five acts written in verse by Edmond Rostand, first performed in 1897. It is considered a masterpiece of French Romantic theatre. The play is loosely inspired by the real Cyrano de Bergerac (1619-1655), a Gascon poet and duelist, but the story is largely fictionalized.

== MAIN CHARACTERS ==

- Cyrano de Bergerac: Poet, philosopher, and exceptional duelist, a cadet in the Gascon Guards. He has a very large nose about which he is deeply self-conscious, but compensates with extraordinary eloquence, bravery, and wit. He is secretly in love with Roxane.

- Roxane: Cyrano's cousin, beautiful and refined, passionate about fine language. She falls in love with Christian, but in reality it is Cyrano's soul she loves without knowing it.

- Christian de Neuvillette: A handsome young cadet in the Guards, in love with Roxane, but awkward with words. He teams up with Cyrano to win Roxane's heart.

- Count de Guiche: A powerful nobleman, in love with Roxane, jealous of Cyrano and Christian.

- Le Bret: Cyrano's loyal friend.

- Ragueneau: A pastry chef and poet, a friend of Cyrano.

== ACT-BY-ACT SUMMARY ==

--- ACT I: The Hotel de Bourgogne Theatre ---

We are introduced to Cyrano, a flamboyant and feared figure whose physical appearance (his enormous nose) contrasts sharply with the greatness of his soul. That evening, he drives an actor off the stage for being too mediocre. He confides to his friend Le Bret his secret love for Roxane, his cousin. But he is convinced that his nose makes him unworthy of being loved. Roxane arranges to meet him the next day.

--- ACT II: The Poets' Cookshop ---

Roxane confesses to Cyrano that she is in love with Christian, a handsome cadet who is too shy to speak to her. She asks Cyrano to protect him. Christian arrives in the company and is clumsy, attempting to mock Cyrano's nose — which Cyrano forgives when he learns Christian is Roxane's chosen one. The two men form an alliance: Christian will lend his face, Cyrano his voice and words.

--- ACT III: The Balcony ---

This is the most famous act of the play. At night, beneath Roxane's balcony, Cyrano whispers to Christian the words to say. Then, when Christian can no longer continue, Cyrano takes his place in the darkness and declares his love to Roxane in his own name, without her knowing it. Roxane, moved by the beautiful words, agrees to marry Christian. The Count de Guiche, jealous, sends Christian and Cyrano to the siege of Arras to get rid of them.

--- ACT IV: The Siege of Arras ---

The cadets are starving in the trenches. Cyrano, risking his life, crosses enemy lines every morning to deliver letters to Roxane — letters signed by Christian but written in his own hand. Roxane arrives on the battlefield, moved by the beauty of these letters. She reveals to Christian that even if he were ugly, she would love him for his soul. Christian realizes that Roxane truly loves Cyrano, and asks him to reveal the truth. Before Cyrano can act, Christian is killed in battle. Cyrano chooses to remain silent forever and keep the secret.

--- ACT V: The Convent --- (Fifteen years later)

Roxane, inconsolable, has withdrawn to a convent. Cyrano visits her every week. One day, gravely wounded in an ambush (his enemies had a heavy beam dropped on his head), he arrives dying at their usual appointment. Roxane reads him the last letter from "Christian". Cyrano recites it from memory, in the darkness. Roxane then understands that it was always him who wrote those letters, that it was him she loved all along. But it is too late: Cyrano dies, true to himself, proclaiming that he takes his panache with him.

== MAIN THEMES ==

- Impossible love and sacrifice: Cyrano renounces love out of fear of rejection.
- The duality of body and soul: The contrast between physical ugliness and inner beauty.
- Honour and panache: Cyrano embodies a certain ideal of nobility of spirit, pride, and courage in the face of adversity.
- Jealousy and power: The Count de Guiche represents those who use power to obtain what they desire.

== FAMOUS QUOTE ==

"What I have done, I have done through myself alone."

== CONCLUSION ==

Cyrano de Bergerac is a work that touches on the universal: love, pride, sacrifice, and death. The character of Cyrano, at once ridiculous and sublime, is one of the most endearing figures in French literature. The play ends on a tragic yet luminous note, with Cyrano dying on his feet, faithful to his ideals until the very end.
